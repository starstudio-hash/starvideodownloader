Why video files get corrupted

Video corruption happens more often than you'd expect. Files can become unplayable due to interrupted transfers, camera shutdowns mid-recording, SD card errors, incomplete downloads, or software crashes during editing. The result: videos that won't open, play with glitches, have no audio, or show a black screen.

Common sources of corrupted videos include:

  • GoPro / action cameras — battery dies mid-recording, leaving a truncated file
  • Drone footage (DJI) — signal loss or emergency landing corrupts the recording
  • Dashcam recordings — power cut or SD card failure
  • iPhone/iPad recordings — storage full or app crash during recording
  • Screen recordings — system crash during capture
  • Incomplete downloads — network interruption leaves a partial file

What types of corruption can be fixed?

Missing moov atom

The most common corruption. The moov atom contains the video's index and is normally written at the end of recording. If recording stops abruptly, this index is missing and the file won't play at all.

Broken container structure

The MP4/MOV container's internal structure is damaged. The video data exists but the file's "table of contents" is broken.

Corrupt frames

Individual frames within the video are damaged, causing visual glitches, green blocks, or freezing at certain points.

Audio/video desync

The audio and video tracks have drifted apart, causing lip sync issues or audio that plays at the wrong time.

Truncated files

The file was cut short — the video plays partially then stops, or shows the wrong duration.

Metadata damage

File metadata is corrupted — wrong duration, missing codec info, or incorrect resolution reported.

How the Multi-Stage Repair Works

Star Video Downloader uses a 6-stage repair pipeline powered by ffmpeg. Each stage tries a different repair strategy, and the tool automatically advances to the next stage if the previous one doesn't fully fix the file:

  1. Container rebuild — remuxes the file to fix structural issues (instant)
  2. Faststart optimization — moves the moov atom to the correct position (instant)
  3. Error-tolerant remux — copies streams while ignoring minor errors (fast)
  4. Corrupt frame discard — skips damaged frames while preserving good ones (fast)
  5. Audio resync — re-synchronizes audio and video tracks (medium)
  6. Full re-encode — re-encodes the entire file as a last resort (slower, best recovery chance)

Stages 1–4 preserve original quality with no re-encoding. Stage 6 uses high-quality settings (CRF 18) to minimize quality loss while recovering as much content as possible.
